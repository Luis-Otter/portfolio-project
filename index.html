Signs/Symptoms

Understanding mental health is the first step toward caring for yourself and others. Just like your physical health, your mental health deserves attention and care.

What it looks like:

Constant worry or fear that something bad will happen, even if there’s no real danger. Teens may experience physical symptoms like headaches or stomachaches due to anxiety.


Signs to look out for:

  • Always fearing the worst-case scenario in situations (e.g., tests, friendships).
  • Avoiding situations that make them nervous, like social gatherings or public speaking.
  • Physical symptoms such as racing heart, sweating, dizziness, or feeling shaky.
  • Difficulty sleeping or frequently waking up due to worry.

What it looks like:

Feeling sad or down for long periods, even when there doesn’t seem to be an obvious reason. Teens may also feel more easily frustrated or irritable.


Signs to look out for:

  • Frequent crying or emotional outbursts.
  • Easily annoyed by others or getting upset over small issues.
  • Losing interest in activities they used to enjoy, such as sports, hobbies, or socializing with friends.
  • Feeling “empty” or numb, without any clear feelings of joy or excitement.

What it looks like:

Sudden and intense mood swings, often going from happiness to deep sadness or anger within a short period of time.


Signs to look out for:

  • Extreme highs (feeling euphoric, overly energetic) followed by extreme lows (feeling worthless, overly tired).
  • Talking very fast and being overly enthusiastic one moment, then withdrawing or shutting down the next.
  • Difficulty managing emotions, leading to unpredictable behavior or decisions.
  • Feeling overwhelmed by their emotions, which may lead to tears, yelling, or withdrawal.

What it looks like:

Avoiding social interactions, even with close friends or family members. Teens might isolate themselves in their room, decline invitations to social activities, or show little interest in spending time with loved ones.


Signs to look out for:

  • Spending excessive time alone or avoiding interactions that used to bring them joy.
  • Showing disinterest in friendships or romantic relationships.
  • Becoming more withdrawn in conversations, avoiding eye contact, or being unresponsive when spoken to.
  • Refusing to attend family events or social gatherings that they once enjoyed.

What it looks like:

Either sleeping too much (hypersomnia) or not being able to sleep enough (insomnia). Sleep problems can make it harder for teens to focus and function during the day.


Signs to look out for:

  • Struggling to fall asleep or stay asleep through the night.
  • Feeling tired all the time, even after sleeping for many hours.
  • Frequently falling asleep in class or feeling drowsy during the day.
  • Staying up late at night and having trouble waking up in the morning, which can lead to missing school or other responsibilities.

Coping Strategies


  • Talk About It

    Talking to a trusted friend, parent, teacher, or counselor can help. It may seem difficult, but opening up often provides relief and perspective.


  • Mindfulness

    Deep breathing exercises, guided meditations, or simply taking 5-10 minutes to relax and clear your mind can reduce stress and anxiety.


  • Exercise

    Physical activity boosts your mood by releasing endorphins. Whether it’s going for a walk, doing yoga, or playing a sport, find what works for you.


  • Routine

    Regular sleep patterns, balanced meals, and staying hydrated can improve your mood and energy levels.


  • Limit Social Media

    Social media can sometimes contribute to feelings of inadequacy or anxiety. Set limits on your screen time, and don’t compare your life to what you see online.

Where to Find Help

If you feel like you're struggling with your mental health or you’re concerned about a friend, there are resources available to you. Here’s where you can start:

  • School Counselor

    Most schools have counselors trained to help with mental health issues. They are a great first step in getting the support you need.

  • Local Therapists

    Find a therapist near you by searching online or asking your doctor for a recommendation.

FAQs:

  • What should I do if I’m feeling overwhelmed?

    Start by taking a deep breath and focusing on one task at a time. Reach out to someone you trust, and if the feeling persists, consider speaking with a counselor.


  • How do I tell my parents I need help with my mental health?

    Choose a time when you can talk calmly. Be honest about how you’re feeling and let them know that seeking professional help could be beneficial for you.


  • What if my friend doesn’t want help?

    You can encourage them, but ultimately it’s their choice to seek help. If you’re concerned about their safety, speak to a trusted adult immediately.
